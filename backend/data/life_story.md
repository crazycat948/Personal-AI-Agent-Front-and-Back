# Life Story

## Childhood

Yifan grew up in Changsha, China as an only child. Because of this, he received a great deal of attention and care from his family during childhood.

He believes his earliest sense of self-awareness began around the age of five, when he first entered elementary school. During his early school years, teachers often described him as a creative student. However, he struggled with maintaining focus and often lacked initiative when it came to studying.

At one point he briefly attended a Go (Weiqi) training class, but he quickly realized he did not enjoy it. Sitting still for long periods of time was difficult for him, and he stopped attending not long after.

Later he joined a robotics training program, which he found much more interesting. This was also his first exposure to programming and technology.

Throughout most of elementary school, Yifan spent a large portion of his time training with a swimming team. The training was physically demanding, but it helped him build discipline and perseverance. More importantly, he formed strong friendships with teammates, and those friendships remain some of his happiest childhood memories.

During middle school he attended a boarding school, which limited his participation in extracurricular activities. His academic performance was still not particularly strong, partly due to the intense academic pressure and partly due to his own difficulty staying focused on schoolwork.

Despite this, he remembers middle school as a very lively and energetic period of life. Socially he was outgoing and playful, and it was probably the most carefree and boisterous stage of his life.

## Moving to the United States

When Yifan moved from China to the United States, the differences in culture and environment were overwhelming at first.

Fortunately, many of the first people he met in the United States were kind and welcoming. He was never bullied, and classmates were often willing to help him. However, because he could not understand English well and was not used to the outgoing communication style of American students, he sometimes avoided conversations altogether.

Looking back, he feels that he may have unintentionally come across as rude by rejecting conversations simply because he felt uncomfortable speaking English.

Over time, this habit of avoiding communication made him more introverted than he had been before.

From a systems perspective, he also noticed that many aspects of life in the United States felt more structured and organized compared to what he had experienced in China. This actually made daily life somewhat easier to adapt to.

However, the area where he lived in Houston felt relatively far from the city center, and the lack of public transportation meant that he often lived a very repetitive routine: going only between school and home.

During this period he often felt that life in the United States was somewhat boring and isolated.

Things improved later in high school after he learned how to drive and began forming closer friendships. Having more independence and mobility made his life feel much more open.

## Before College

Before entering college, Yifan admits that he had very little understanding of what university life actually meant.

Even after graduating high school, he did not clearly know what the university he would attend was like or where it was located. Much of the application process had been handled through an agency rather than through his own research.

At first he intended to study business. Later he considered chemical engineering. However, after discussions with his father, he eventually switched to computer science.

His father believed that business might not fit his personality, and that chemical engineering could also be unstable due to industry downturns at the time. Computer science seemed more aligned with long-term technological trends.

Although the decision was somewhat practical rather than passion-driven at the time, it later became an important turning point in his academic direction.

## Early College Struggles (University of Nebraska–Lincoln)

Yifan began his college studies at the University of Nebraska–Lincoln.

The experience was extremely difficult for several reasons.

First, Nebraska felt very different from Texas. The population was less diverse, and he sometimes sensed subtle ethnic bias. For example, once when he went to pick up a package, the staff member checking his ID jokingly said that if he were white, they would not need to check his ID.

There were also moments when local students looked at him with visible curiosity, which made him feel somewhat out of place.

The environment itself was also challenging. Nebraska winters were extremely cold, with snow sometimes reaching knee height. Without a car, he had to walk long distances to get to class. The days became dark very early, which often made the atmosphere feel gloomy.

Academically, he struggled as well. Before entering university he had almost no exposure to computer science and little real interest in it. Combined with poor study habits developed during high school, he found even introductory material very difficult.

He remembers taking only three classes during his first semester and still feeling completely lost in lectures. At the time, he also lacked the habit of reviewing or preparing outside of class.

After a year of struggling academically and personally, he eventually decided to leave the university.

## Reset and Reflection (Houston Community College)

After leaving the University of Nebraska–Lincoln, Yifan returned to Houston.  
At first he did not immediately recover from the disappointment of his previous experience. For a while he remained somewhat discouraged and uncertain about his direction.

However, during this period he experienced several important changes.

For the first time, he found a part-time job. Working allowed him to see how society actually operates outside of school, and it gave him a new perspective on responsibility and independence. During this time he also met several people who became meaningful to him.

Gradually, he began to reflect seriously on his past decisions. He realized that many of his earlier struggles were connected to habits and discipline rather than ability.

One of the first changes he made was improving his physical health. During his time at Nebraska he had developed unhealthy eating habits and his weight had increased significantly, reaching around 110 kilograms. Determined to change, he began focusing on weight loss and eventually reduced his weight to about 80 kilograms. Later he also began regular fitness training.

At the same time, his academic habits improved. He started studying more consistently and developed better discipline toward coursework. In his final two semesters at Houston Community College, he took a heavy course load of twelve classes and earned straight A's.

Despite these improvements, this period was still psychologically difficult. Yifan often felt intense pressure and self-doubt. Seeing many of his peers already attending universities made him feel that he had fallen behind in life.

The semester when he was applying to transfer universities was particularly stressful. He was deeply afraid that he might not be accepted anywhere and that his academic path might end at community college.

Then, on his birthday, he received his first university acceptance letter—from the University of Texas at Tyler. Although it was not his ideal destination, the offer brought him enormous relief because it meant he would at least have somewhere to continue his education.

Shortly afterward, he received an admission offer from the University of Texas at Dallas (UTD). This news was extremely exciting for him and marked the beginning of a new stage in his academic journey.
